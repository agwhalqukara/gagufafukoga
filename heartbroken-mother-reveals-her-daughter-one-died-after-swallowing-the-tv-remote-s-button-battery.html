Heartbroken mother reveals her daughter, one, died after swallowing the TV remote's button battery

August 2024 · 6 minute read

A heartbroken mother is raising awareness about the dangers of button batteries after her 17-month-old daughter died from swallowing one. 

Trista Hamsmith, from Texas, lost her daughter Reese on December 17 after three grueling months spent in the hospital where the toddler received several surgeries and was put on breathing aids after swallowing the battery at home. 

Reese swallowed the family’s TV remote control’s button battery in October, which burned a hole in her throat, making it more difficult for her to breathe and eat. 

Trista, who created the Pray For Reese Hamsmith Facebook Page during her daughter’s stay in the hospital told Today that her story needed to be told and could have been avoided. 

Reese Hamsmith, pictured, 17-months-old, died on December 17 2020, three months after a button battery got stuck in her throat and burnt a hole through her esophagus (pictured before)

Reese’s had trouble breathing and ingesting food due to the hole and spent several months in hospital going through several operations after she was put on a gastronomy tube and a ventilator, pictured

Reese had been a bubbly, happy child before the incident, but when Trista caught her wheezing at home, she took her to the doctor.

The doctor said the 17-month-old was suffering from a bad cold and sent mother and daughter home.

But Trista’s heart stopped when she realized a battery from her TV’s remote control was missing, and rushed Reese to the emergency room, where X-rays confirmed she had swallowed the button battery, and that it had burnt a hole in her esophagus.

Reese had emergency surgery to remove the battery from her throat, but much damage had been done. Once swallowed, the battery had started to erode and to burn the wall of the baby’s throat. 

An X-ray showed that the coin-shaped battery was stuck in the toddler’s esophagus. Reese had swallowed the TV remote control’s battery

Heartbroken mother Trista Hamsmith, pictured with Reese, is now raising awareness about the dangers of button batteries and advocating for safer batteries and battery compartments 

Dr. Emily Durkin, medical director of children’s surgery at Helen DeVos Children’s Hospital in Grand Rapids, Michigan, who did not operate on Reese, said a button battery could have severe repercussions on a child’s health. 

She explained that the esophagus comprises two narrow areas at the lower and upper ends, where batteries can get trapped.  

She said that when a battery gets stuck in these narrowings, the esophagus’ walls can collapse against the button battery and its back wall, causing electric current to flow through the esophageal tissue, killing it and burning it. 

Before the incident, Reese had been a bubbly child, pictured. But Trista said her daughter was not herself after being put on a ventilator to help her breathing in hospital 

Why are button batteries dangerous? 

The number of fatal injuries stustained after swallowing a button battery has increased in recent years. 

They are a choking hazard and their chemical can cause severe damage to a child’s body, especially if it gets struck in their esophagus.  

Burns and life-threatening complications can occur if batteries aren’t removed from the esophagus within 2 hours.

If a child swallowed a battery, they should be taken to an emergency room as soon as possible. 

Batteries should never been left out in the house and should be stored safely away from children. 

Items such as remote control can be safely tapped to avoid children opening them. 

Source: Poison.org

After Reese’s operation, she completed a short stay in the hospital before being sent home, but the family was back a few days later when her condition started to worsen again. 

‘We found out that a fistula had been created, which is like a passageway,’ Trista said. 

The distraught mother explained the hole in Reese’s throat meant the air was not going where it needed to, and food and drinks were also diverted from the child’s stomach. 

After gastric surgery where Reese was fitted with a tube to bypass the hole, she was sent back to her room on a ventilator. 

‘That morning was the last morning that we saw her as herself,’ Trista said.

After some more time in hospital, Reese went through another operation to repair the fistula in her throat, and the 17-month-old started to improve. 

Trista initially thought Reese had caught a bad cold when the child started wheezing at home in late October (pictured in hospital)

‘The surgery went great and then from there it was just more waiting, more resting, more healing,’ Trista said. 

‘A few weeks later they tried to take her off the ventilator and she did great.’ 

However, this moment of relief was brief, and Reese’s condition started to deteriorate again. 

Trista recalled how she left her daughter’s room for a few minute one day in December and came back to find Reese surrounded by doctors trying to revive her. 

The 17-months-old had to go through several operations to help with her nutrition and breathing but was eventually too weak to be taken off her ventilator 

Reese at 14 months old, before the battery incident. Trista said she prayed for her daughter to get better but doctors were unable to revive her 

‘I heard them say “starting compressions” and she was gone for about eight to ten minutes,’ Trista said. ‘We were able to get her back. Ultimately they said she wasn’t strong enough yet.’

After this incident, doctors tried to take Reese of the ventilator but were not successful, and later decided to perform a tracheotomy on her to help her breathe. 

Trista said she was terrified to see her daughter go through another surgery, but was also hopeful it would help Reese get better. 

Three days after the operation, which went well, Reese started to struggle and her vital signs plummeted in spite of doctors’ repeated efforts to keep her alive. 

Reese in hospital. Trista has now created an organization in Reese’s name to advocate for safer batteries in hopes of saving the lives of other children 

 The heartbroken mother said she prayed Reese would get better, but that her baby daughter died on December 17 after doctors tried to revive her for 30 to 40 minutes. 

Trista is now raising awareness against button battery, warning against their danger to children. 

She said she was hoping manufacturers would start creating safer batteries and batteries compartments – notably by putting screws on battery compartment for button batteries so that children can’t open them. 

Dr Durkin agrees that the batteries are the most dangerous kind, because they’re the size of a quarter, and shouldn’t be made.

 Nearly three months after her daughter’s passing, Trista is putting together an organization called Reese’s purpose to advocate for safer batteries, saying she felt it was a fitting way to remember her child.
